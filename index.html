The Problem

Your users expect invisible camera switches

Every zoom tap is a UX test. Most Android phones fail it — and users notice, even if they can't name why.

Alignment

Geometric jump

Objects shift position abruptly at the cut. The user sees the entire scene "snap" left or right — a hard discontinuity that breaks immersion. This is the most fundamental MCT failure: misalignment between the two camera views at the exact moment of switch. Even sub-pixel misalignment is perceptible on high-res displays.

Perceptual

Color & exposure flash

Each sensor has its own white balance, AEC, and tone curve. At the cut, the scene temperature and brightness jump — a "flash" effect users consistently report as the most jarring artifact. MCT must match not just geometry but the perceptual appearance: luminance, contrast, and color temperature across the seam.

Motion

Motion discontinuity

Spatial alignment is not enough. The optical flow field must also be consistent across cameras — or the user feels a stutter mid-pan, as if playback skipped a frame. This is especially visible during video recording where the hand is constantly moving, even subtly.

Temporal

Post-switch settling

A perfect first frame is not enough. After switching, the new camera's AWB, AF, and AEC need time to converge — causing visible drift, breathing, or flicker for hundreds of milliseconds. Users perceive this as "the phone figuring itself out" — a cheap, unpolished feeling that erodes confidence in the camera system.

Vidhance MCT addresses all four dimensions above — adapting at runtime without factory calibration.

Hardware limitations beyond MCT scope:

Optical FOV change · native resolution difference · AF convergence speed · exposure convergence · rolling shutter · DoF mismatch · sensor noise profile

Technical context

The geometry between cameras changes constantly

Camera alignment is not a static property. Three forces continuously corrupt it.

🏭

Manufacturing tolerance

Every unit rolls off the line slightly different. No two phones have identical camera geometry.

🌡️

Thermal drift

Heat from CPU and battery causes micro-deformation of the chassis. Alignment shifts during a recording session.

📱

Physical impact

One drop permanently shifts sensor position. A calibration done at the factory is now wrong — forever.

A correct MCT solution must account for all three — at runtime, on every device, for the lifetime of the phone.

How competitor MCT works

1

Manual optical alignment

Each unit placed in a jig on the production line. A technician (or robot) physically aligns the cameras and measures offset.

2

Offset tables burned in

Fixed correction values written to firmware. The phone applies these values for its entire lifetime.

3

Shipped

No recalibration possible without a factory return.

Fatal flaw 1 — expensive production

Every unit requires a dedicated calibration step on the line. Adds time, cost, and a failure mode (mis-calibrated units) to manufacturing.

Fatal flaw 2 — fragile in the field

A single drop shifts the geometry. The burned-in offset table no longer matches reality. The user experiences degraded MCT quality for the rest of the device's life.

Calibration at the factory is a ceiling, not a solution.
